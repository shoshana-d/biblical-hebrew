How Tanakh verbs work

To start with, because we are working backwards from the English translation, we need to know some things about verbs, and particularly, how verbs in Hebrew are different from verbs in English

As a reminder about what verbs are, here are some verses from the English translation of Genesis chapter 3, verses 1 to 5 with the verbs highlighted:

Now the serpent was most cunning of all the beasts of the field that the Lord God had made. And he said to the woman, "Though God said you shall not eat from any tree of the garden-" And the woman said to the serpent, "From the fruit of the garden's trees we may eat but from the fruit of the tree in the midst of the garden God has said, 'You shall not eat from it and you shall not touch it, lest you die.'" And the serpent said to the woman, "You shall not be doomed to die. For God knows that on the day you eat of it your eyes will be opened and you will become as gods knowing good and evil."
(Translation by Robert Alter, "The Five Books of Moses", 2004)

These are the main differences we will learn in this lesson:

  • Word order
    • In English the verb comes second: Rebecca saw the cat.
    • In Hebrew the verb often comes first: Saw Rebecca the cat.
  • Hebrew verb packs a punch, contains much more information than English verb.
    • Hebrew verb is one word
    • English translation is often 2, 3, or even 4 words
      • Some examples from the text above:

We will use the verb "say" in examples. There's lots of talking in the Tanakh: God talks to people, people talk to God, the prophets talk to the Israelites, people talk to each other, and even some animals talk (the serpent to Eve, the ass to Balaam (Numbers 22, 28 and 30), so it's no surprise that the most frequently occurring verb in the Tanakh is "say" from the root אמר.

Let's think about how this verb works in English. There only a few forms of the actual verb:

  • say (to say, I say, you say, we say, they say, I will say, I used to say, did you say?)
  • says (he says, she says, the boy says, Rachel says)
  • said (I said, you said, he said, she said, we said, they said, the boy said, Rachel said)
  • saying (she was saying, they were saying)
but there may be one or more extra words which are also part of the verb (will, shall, had, have, will be, may, lest)

In Tanakh Hebrew, verbs have lots of different forms.

Every Hebrew verb is based on a 3 letter "root", for example the root אמר. Vowels are added to the root letters and letter(s) at the beginning (prefixes) and/or at the end (suffixes) to create the different forms of the verb.

Why are there so many different forms of Hebrew verbs when English verbs only have a few forms?

  • Biblical Hebrew verbs are always a single word. This word often corresponds to 2 or more words in the English translation.
  • includes information about who did the action
  • so sometimes translation has "he", "she" etc when this is part of the verb
• Vayomer from root amr to speak, indicates that a single male spoke, usually translated in past tense • Vatomer indicates a sinlge female spoke • In English “said” same for male or female speakers
