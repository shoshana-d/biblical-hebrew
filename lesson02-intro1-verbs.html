Overview 1 How Tanakh Hebrew is different from English: verbs

  • Biblical Hebrew verbs are always a single word. This word often corresponds to 2 or more words in the English translation.
  • The verb often comes before the subject (Spoke God to Moses.)

There's lots of talking in the Tanakh. God speaks to people, people speak to God, the prophets speak to the Israelites, people speak to each other, so we’ll start by learning a sentence which occurs frequently in the Torah:

An example which occurs many times in the Torah, for example :

Exodus 4:4 וַיֹּ֤אמֶר יְהוָה֙ אֶל־מֹשֶׁ֔ה God said to Moses

The individual Hebrew words are:

Order of words in sentences is often verb subject

  • In English the word order is usually subject-verb-object, so that the verb comes after the subject:
    The girl
    (subject)
    saw
    (verb)
    the cat
    (object)
  • In Tanakh Hebrew the word order is often verb-subject-object, that is, the verb often comes before the subject
    Saw
    (verb)
    the girl
    (subject)
    the cat
    (object)

    (although this sentence doesn't occur in the Tanakh because sadly there is no mention of cats in the Tanakh)

can often use this to figure out which word is the verb

vayomer, vatomer always come before the subject

Tanakh verbs from 3 consonant roots

vayomer, vatomer Verbs 3 consonant roots, vowels, suffixes and prefixes added this is amr, much more in next lesson • Vayomer from root amr to speak, indicates that a single male spoke, usually translated in past tense • Vatomer indicates a sinlge female spoke • In English “said” same for male or female speakers

may have prefix like vayomer, vatomer

may have suffix, example not translated

may have both prefix and suffix, vayomru

may have neither, just added vowels amar, dictionary form

Tanakh verbs contain more information than English verbs about who is doing the action, so don’t need personal pronouns, these are mostly used for emphasis

Because the Tanakh uses a very small number of words

Good news is that the most frequent account for a large proportion of the text, so that by learning to recognize the most frequent will be able torecognize one or more words in mearly every verse

-אֶל words/w008_el

• El preposition, often attached to next word – this is very helpful • Hyphens in Tanakh Hebrew different from in English

-אֶל words/w008_el is a very frequently occurring preposition meaning "to". It is usually joined to the next word with a hyphen. When it is not joined to the next word it is written אֵל words/w008_el

yes, אֵל words/w008_el is also one of the (less frequent) words for God

Can use words you know to figure out words you have't learnt yet.
